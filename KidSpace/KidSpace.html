Kid Space

Lead UX Designer 2015-2017





Start from a field study

During this field study, we shared several novel technologies with participants (who are relatively open to new technologies), ask their opinions about new technologies and how they would like to use them.
One finding is that people are more open to technology than before. And they hope technology could help their kids learn through play.
Thus, kid space is one of our focus area generated from this field study.





Brainstorming

What should be included in a kid smart space?
We listed elements in kids' play space, explore the possible combination of attractive play experience and the technologies we are interested in.






Generate scenarios

Based on brainstorming discussion, we generated 15 ideas for kid space.

To evaluate these ideas, we brought stakeholders to vote on ideas based on following criterias:
  • Compelling
  • Scalability
  • Tech feasibility
  • Multimodality
  • Physical immersion
  • Originality

    • One of the ideas we generated





    Kid Space = Multimodality + Project Mapping Technology + AR + Play Space

    It provides an environment that delivers fun, educational AR content in a way that is responsive to children’s actions, utterances and emotions.
    While VR/AR headset is not recommended for kid under 12, our AR projecting solution could adapt to younger kids and encourage them to move around.

    The advantages of Kid Space system:
  • Augments a space, allowing children to retain focus on reality, including the real objects in the space
  • Provides projected output across an entire room
  • Dynamically responds to people and objects in the environment
  • Is compelling for children to learn through play




    • Kid Space is directed by a virtual agent: Oscar

    Oscar is a projected agent who can guide kids learning through play. Oscar understands the surrounding environment and has auto speech recognition capability, so he can respond to people and ojects in the environment.

    User Study

    Two preliminary studies evaluated Kid Space with children 5 to 8 years old.
    Because technology is not ready at this time, we tested the experiences using a “Wizard of Oz” methodology

    Study 1

    The following photos and previous video are from Study 1.

    It was loosely structured and exploratory , allowing the acquisition of qualitative data to indicate children’s responses to the interactive character and of parent ratings of the system after they observed the session.

    The wizard wizards in the control room spoke freely and naturally with the children through Oscar. The wizards did not rigorously follow a script but led the children through the planned activities such as "do math with Oscar", "Red light/ green light".

    The study was composed of five sessions.
    In each session 2 parents from different families each brought 1 or 2 children.
    Thus each session had 2 parents and 2-4 children for a total of 16 children and 10 parentsparents.

    Children are playing with Virtual Agent "Oscar"



    Results from Study 1

    Qualitative indicators from the first study showed that children engaged with Oscar in many ways:
  • Carried on conversations with Oscar
  • Answered math challenges
  • Responded to Oscar’s suggestions to participate in energetic dancing and jumping
  • Emoted in response to Oscar’s “wearing” outfits that the children colored themselves
  • Touched Oscar’s projection, pretending to feed him, to tickle him, or to experiment with the projection’s reflectance on their hands or on paper




    • Study 2

    Study 2 was more tightly structured than Study 1 and focused on evaluating two aspects of Kid Space : the AR presentation style and the agent’s awareness of the physicality of the children.
    The study included 8 sessions, each with 2 parents, usually from different families, and 2 children, for a total of 16 children and 16 parents.
    The parents watched passively during the session and then responded to questionnaires at the end of each session.

    Results from Study 2

    Study 2 found that children engaged with a projected agent at a closer distance than with a television.

    Parents showed a preference for a projected AR agent over an agent on a television or a standard projection.
    The reasons for the parents’ preferences included that children remained more mentally present in their environment with the AR projection compared to a television or floating projection.
